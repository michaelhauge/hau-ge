Scalability is the same thing as scale, right?

In my opinion, not quite. The word 'scalability' is thrown around a lot and even more frequently in the last dozen years or so with so many internet startups. Definitions of such terms are often subjective, but my understanding is this: **scalability is the measurement of the capacity of a system to handle growth with as few additional costs as possible.**

The increased growth comes from more throughput (volume of units), more complexity (of people and processes), or more infrastructure (required to support the two previous factors). Scalability is measured by the maintenance (or ideally, increase) of profit margins as the company grows. Companies that are 'ultimately scalable' are said to have unimpeded growth and unimpeded improvement in quality as the number of units/people/services delivered increases.

The paragon of scalability might be Baidu or Google. For every additional search term entered, there isn't a large additional cost to perform that service. In fact, these services get better and better the more people use it. If they get bigger their product actually becomes more valuable due to network effects and thus advertisers should be willing to pay more. If three hundred people use Google, the search results are less helpful (and therefore valuable) than if three billion people use Google.

A different example: McDonald's. For each additional hamburger that a franchise creates, there isn't much more that the company needs to do to support the quality of the experience for the end consumer. McDonald's could open 10 more stores tomorrow and it would be relatively straightforward to do so without incurring many costs aside from the upfront construction of a new location. Compare that to a local dumpling shop with one storefront. The dumpling shop is likely to do more work to serve the 50th customer of the day on a comparative basis than McDonald's does. And they certainly need to do more work to serve the 5,000th customer of the day. Whether the 50th or 5,000th burger, the quality is also more likely to be similar at McDonald's than at the local dumpling shop.

## Three things people get wrong about scalability

There might be a few sticking points here that some people may hit. By seeing Baidu, Google, and McDonald's as larger companies that 'operate at scale,' they equate "size" with "scalability." Let's unpack:

**1. "Operating at scale" is not the same as scalability.** The phrase is most often interpreted to mean *"operating at (a larger) scale (than you are now)."* A colleague may ask you, *"Will this work at scale?"* The inferred question is, *"Will your plan work at 10 times or 100 times the amount of users?"* This is an amazing and forward-thinking question. But it doesn't get at the heart of the scalability question: are margins sustained or improving as the number of users increases?

**2. Scale is relative, not absolute.** To a smaller player in the industry, LearningLeaders may 'operate at scale.' To LearningLeaders, a single university may 'operate at scale.' To a single university, New Oriental may 'operate at scale.' There isn't a certain number at which 'scale' is achieved — it's a matter of relative size when compared to other contemporary or competitive businesses.

**3. Size does not equal profit.** If a company has one billion RMB in revenues, it must make more money than a company that generates half that in revenues, correct? Possible, but not necessarily true. The classic counter-example is Apple. In 2018, Apple only accounted for one third of the global smartphone industry revenues, but it accounted for two-thirds of the industry's profits. Walmart has double the revenue of Apple, but only one tenth of the profits. So 'operating at scale' does not necessarily mean anything about gross or net margins.

## Is scalability always a good thing?

Scalability within a company is not necessary in all cases. However, it is necessary if a company wants to continue to compensate the team members more generously over time, to grow the operations and simultaneously invest in improvements, and to make an ever-larger impact in the world.

There are plenty of amazing businesses who thrive with non-scalable economics. Think of the Japanese ramen shop owner who painstakingly perfects their craft over forty years and only opens one ramen shop. Think of the sneaker artist who paints each shoe brushstroke by brushstroke. Think of the single doctor practitioner who sees patients on their own. These comparatively bespoke experiences don't necessarily reflect an inherent scalability within the business model, but rather a desire to maximize quality of every interaction at the expense of margins. (Yet there exist ramen chains, sneaker brands, and medical groups that operate in a scalable fashion.)

To be candid, some days I feel like this is what I would love to do — to only work one-on-one with students for the rest of my life to bring them the maximum benefit I can possibly offer them individually. It feels more natural, in a way. Then I remember that what we're doing at LearningLeaders isn't something that should be hidden under a rock. It's an experience that needs to see the light of day and one that would benefit so many more students if we shared it with them.

So scalability isn't a panacea. It's not a cure-all. But in our case, I do believe it is necessary.

## Six ingredients of scalability

Reverse-engineering scalability from its lagging indicator (profitability) reveals a handful of factors that show up consistently:

### 1. Clarity of market focus

The least scalable situation is one in which the company is completely reactive to customers and will sell to anyone who walks through the door. The largest and most profitable companies in the world have a clear idea of who their ideal customers are and create products, services, and experiences for those key personas. Apple, Samsung, Royal Dutch Shell — all have a very clear idea of who their customers are and you better believe they are NOT reactive in their customer selection.

### 2. Customer-centric, not sales-centric

A sales-centric process is designed around explicit actions for salespeople. It depends on specific 'star' talent, it is more expensive because it actually requires that salesperson's time, and it's possible for salespeople to take shortcuts to get to the finish line rather than address all the concerns of the potential customer.

A customer-centric process is designed around 'helping the customer buy.' Each phase is designed to be cognizant of the emotions and decisions each potential customer will feel and make at a specific step within the buying journey. It's less dependent on 'star' salespeople, the process educates the customer throughout (so there are fewer post-sales problems), and it encourages referral business — the most scalable form of customer acquisition out there.

### 3. Outcome-focused, not feature-focused

Customers care about outcomes. That's because outcomes are what actually affect them. The majority of customers don't really care about how much work went into creating the product. They care about what the product or experience does for them. They generally care about the end, not the means.

Limiting risk (variability) and increasing certainty of outcomes the customer desires should be the goal of communicating value. Show the customer that 'A-ha moment' as early and frequently as possible.

### 4. Technology and repeatability

A key enabler of scalability is the copying and moving of digital assets. By driving as much of the daily operations into instantly repeatable processes, we improve our throughput, improve quality standards, lower cognitive load on our team, and move more costs from variable to fixed.

### 5. Parallel, not sequential operations

Operating in parallel means that multiple versions of the same process can happen at the same time. The more parallel processes that can be in existence at once, the better for the business's ability to preserve margins as it grows. Operating sequentially creates a culture of 'stars' where processes grind to a halt if a certain person doesn't execute on their plan.

### 6. Trust and culture

Businesses only move at the speed of trust. The more people within a company trust each other, the faster decisions can be made without approval from the big boss — less-experienced or more junior team members understand and know how the senior management might want them to approach a given situation. Why does a company with a strong culture and high level of trust create more scalability? Because cost of company operations drops. Fewer 'check-ins' and 'checkpoints' along the way.

## Six challenges to scaling

If creating a company that grows and maintains margins was easy, everyone would do it. But of all businesses started in the USA every year, only about 4% reach $1MM Annual Revenue and only 0.4% make it to $10MM. The biggest challenges:

**Variation in output.** The single most critical preventative factor. If McDonald's cannot produce all of the burgers to taste the same, they will violate their brand promise. The way to combat this is to standardize processes of inputs and development, leading to more standardized output. In education, the goal of standardization is not to clip students' creative wings. Rather, it's to provide the baseline skill set and capabilities to everyone, so that each student can then specialize once the foundational skills have been mastered.

**Efficiency of actors.** The more actors there are executing a certain plan, the greater the level of difficulty it is to have them follow the procedure with similar levels of quality. I've made these mistakes many times myself when in the classroom. Frankly, following a centralized lesson plan can feel like the definition of stifling creativity. But if consistently great outcomes are the desired result, then following the designated procedures (essentially checklists) has been proven to increase both quality and consistency. *The Checklist Manifesto* by Atul Gawande is a great book on this.

**Complexity of processes.** The larger the system gets, the more likely it is that variables in one place affect results in another. We must be content in not knowing — focus on the few variables that are the greatest predictors of the desired goal.

**Comprehension of entire systems.** The less detail human decision-makers can maintain over the entire organism. Soon, we'll need to begin to let go more decisively of feeling the need to understand everyone else's workstreams. If everyone has their head in the clouds with their feet on the ground, we'll do amazingly well.

**Morale and powerlessness.** When most people feel powerless over a decision that impacts their life, they can feel demoralized. The Amazon 'two-pizza team' rule is a great rule of thumb: don't have teams bigger than can eat two pizzas in a sitting.

## Why education is locally scalable, not globally scalable

When we examine all of the characteristics of scalability, they are much more challenging to optimize on a global or national level when compared to a local level. Education runs into a particular version of the business 'iron triangle': **Access, Cost, Quality.** You can have any two, but generally not all three. Khan Academy has wide access and is low cost, but each learner has no personalization. Cambridge has high quality, but is high cost and low access. VIPKid sought to break this triangle with one-on-one online tutoring at a middling cost, but the quality (measured by retention) has not held up.

In education, the way a business is set up largely defines its fixed and variable costs. **The greater the proportion of fixed costs, the more scalable the business is likely to be.** MOOCs and Learning Management Systems are highly fixed cost businesses — once created, the marginal cost of an additional learner is near zero. Operating physical schools, one-on-one tutoring, and after-school academies are mostly variable-cost businesses — every additional learner requires another teacher-hour.

Looking at the three highest-profile international school chains for which data is available: **Nord Anglia Education** went public in 2013; more than half of their profit came from China (from only 40% of their overall revenues). Before listing, two-thirds of their profits came from China. **GEMS Education** is estimated to actually be losing money in every market away from the Middle East, where 50 of its 70 schools are located. **Cognita Schools** has 43 of their 75 schools in the UK, with about 75% in or nearby London.

The clear concentration of profits in geographic regions where each chain has a local advantage suggests that 'international school chains' are not actually that international at the unit-economics level. The two major shareable assets across schools in the same region are **sales and marketing** and **curriculum production**. Both are fixed-cost expenses — and when sites are geographically and culturally similar enough, they can genuinely be shared, which compounds margin.

Imagine two campuses: one in Shanghai, one in London. Curriculum differs (different exams, different language needs). Sales and marketing differs (different local customer needs). There isn't a great deal of scalable infrastructure to be shared. The ownership of schools in these two locations doesn't seem scalable.

Playing the example more locally: a school in Shanghai and a school in Hong Kong. More language overlap, more curriculum overlap, more marketing overlap — but still significant. Opening a third in Shenzhen, the overlap is higher again. The closer the campuses are (in the K-12 business), provided they are not cannibalizing each other's demand, the more positive the effect on margins.

So, simply by looking at the operating margins and scalability of LearningLeaders right now, since our advantage is local, **we should continue to exploit that advantage for as long as possible.** All things being equal, if we have 1MM RMB to invest in a LearningCenter, we're going to get the greatest financial return if it's closer, rather than further away from our current operations.

There may be a day five to ten years in the future when we view our physical LearningCenters as fixed costs in themselves and the true operating margins are derived from technology products. This is the transition TAL has been making in plain sight.

## Questions I'm sitting with

This examination of scalability in education has left me with more questions than answers:

1. Which parts of the LL experience can we shift from variable costs to fixed costs? Assessments? Constructive alignment work? Learning Goals? Certain Learning Activities? Declarative or procedural knowledge? Peer interaction? Student-coach interaction? Student-content interaction?
2. How can we potentially leverage asynchronous communication and feedback in group classes? How much more valuable is feedback if received immediately versus within 24 or 48 hours? Within the week?
3. What roles can peer networks play and what elements of the learning experience could be best driven by peers as opposed to professional coaches?

Education businesses that require face-to-face or personal and synchronous service delivery are locally scalable, but not globally scalable. And that's why we're not opening LL centers all over the country and the world this year.
